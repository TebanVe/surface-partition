\begin{abstract}
This document records empirical timing measurements collected by the
\code{RelaxationProfilingState} instrumentation in
\codefile{src/profiling.py} for Phase~1, the projected-gradient-descent
(PGD) relaxation of the $\Gamma$-convergence energy.  Across a
$3\times2$ sweep (penalty weight $\lambda \in \{1.9,2.0,2.1\}$, two
random seeds) on a 30-region torus, a single callback ---
\code{projection}, the orthogonal projection onto the partition and
equal-area constraints --- consumes $86$--$88\% $ of wall time in
\emph{every} run.  The total runtime varies by $4.3\times$
($923$\,s to $3401$\,s) not because of mesh size but because of
\emph{line-search thrashing}: in the slow regime the Armijo backtracking
takes $\sim\!11$ trial steps per iteration, and because the solver
re-projects on every trial step, the projection count explodes.  The
practical conclusion for scaling up $N$ (the number of regions) is that
projection cost, not FEM assembly, is the quantity to attack:
re-projection on backtrack and the per-call inner-iteration count are the
two levers.
\end{abstract}

\section{Overview}
% =========================================================================

Phase~1 minimises the discrete $\Gamma$-convergence energy
\cite{bogosel2023partitions}
\begin{equation}
  E_\varepsilon(u)
  = \varepsilon\, u^{\!\top} K u
  + \tfrac{1}{\varepsilon}\,\big(u^2(1-u)^2\big)^{\!\top} M \,\big(u^2(1-u)^2\big)
  + \text{(penalty)}
  \label{eq:energy}
\end{equation}
over nodal density functions, subject to a partition-of-unity constraint
and an equal-area constraint, using projected gradient descent with an
Armijo backtracking line search.  The solver runs once per mesh
refinement level, warm-starting each level from the previous one by
nearest-neighbour interpolation.

Each major PGD iteration invokes a small set of operations, which become
the canonical callback names used by the profiler:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
\textbf{Callback} & \textbf{Operation} \\
\midrule
\code{matrix\_assembly} & FEM mass/stiffness assembly (once per level) \\
\code{projection}       & orthogonal projection onto constraints (\emph{per trial step}) \\
\code{energy}           & energy evaluation $E_\varepsilon(u)$ (per trial step) \\
\code{gradient}         & energy gradient $\grad E_\varepsilon(u)$ (per iteration) \\
\code{backtrack}        & the whole Armijo \texttt{while} loop (per iteration) \\
\code{init\_interpolate} & inter-level nearest-neighbour interpolation \\
\code{h5\_save}, \code{h5\_flush}, \code{trigger\_check} & I/O and plateau checks \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
This document reports only the \emph{measured cost} of these operations.

\section{Profiling Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% =========================================================================

The class \code{RelaxationProfilingState} (\codefile{src/profiling.py})
accumulates wall-clock time and an invocation count per callback,
\emph{per refinement level}, plus three counters
(\code{major\_iterations}, \code{backtracks\_per\_iter\_total},
\code{projection\_inner\_iters\_total}).  Results are written to
\code{solution/timing\_profile.yaml} when \texttt{-{}-profile} is passed to
\codefile{scripts/find\_surface\_partition.py}, or when
\code{profile: true} is set in the experiment YAML (the mechanism used by
the parameter sweep, which cannot inject CLI flags).  The instrumentation
is zero-overhead when disabled: every hook is a single
\code{if profile is not None} guard, verified to perturb per-level wall
time by $<2\%$.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=blue!4!white, colframe=blue!40!black,
                  title={Nesting of callbacks (matters for accounting)},
                  fontupper=\small]
Two callbacks are recorded \emph{inside} another and must not be
double-counted: \code{energy} and \code{projection} are timed inside
\code{backtrack} (every trial step evaluates both), and
\code{triangle\_areas} is timed inside \code{matrix\_assembly}.  The
non-overlapping per-level sum (all callbacks except \code{energy},
\code{projection}, \code{triangle\_areas}) closes to within
$0.1$--$0.4\%$ of the measured level wall time on every level of every
run below --- confirming the hooks capture essentially all wall time.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Analysis: the Projection Bottleneck}
\label{sec:bottleneck}
% =========================================================================

Two facts together explain every run:

\paragraph{1. Projection is the dominant per-operation cost.}
Each \code{projection} call runs an iterative alternating projection
(Dykstra-style) whose inner-iteration count (\code{mPII} in
Table~\ref{tab:summary}) is $15$--$20$ and grows mildly with mesh size.
Crucially, \code{mPII} is far below the cap of $200$: projection is
\emph{not} hitting its iteration limit.  The cost is therefore driven by
the \emph{number} of projection calls, not by any single call stalling.

\paragraph{2. The line search multiplies the projection count.}
The PGD step is
\begin{equation}
  u^{+} = \Pi_{\mathcal{C}}\!\big(u - s\,\grad E_\varepsilon(u)\big),
  \label{eq:pgd-step}
\end{equation}
and the projection $\Pi_{\mathcal{C}}$ is re-evaluated for \emph{every}
trial step size $s$ during Armijo backtracking.  Hence
\begin{equation}
  \#\,\text{projection calls}
  \;\approx\;
  (\text{major iters}) \times \big(\code{mBT} + 1\big).
  \label{eq:proj-count}
\end{equation}
In the thrashing regime $\code{mBT}\approx 11$, so $\sim\!90\%$ of all
projections are spent on \emph{rejected} trial steps.  This is why the
$\lambda=1.9$ run issues $197\,381$ projection calls versus $10\,902$ for
$\lambda=2.0$ --- an $18\times$ difference that maps directly onto the
$2.6\times$ runtime difference (the thrashing calls are cheaper because
they sit on the coarse level~0).

\begin{remark}[$\lambda$ is not the control variable; the interaction is]
Total wall time is V-shaped in $\lambda$ with a minimum at
$\lambda=2.0$ (Figure~\ref{fig:lambda}), but the effect is not monotone
and is strongly seed-dependent: $\lambda=2.1$ thrashes for seed
13131313 yet is the fastest run for seed 84172851.  Whether the line
search thrashes is a property of the $(\lambda,\text{init})$ pair, not of
$\lambda$ alone.  Only $\lambda=2.0$ was well-behaved for both seeds.
\end{remark}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.80\textwidth]{figures/line_total_wall_vs_lambda.png}
\caption{Total wall time versus $\lambda$ (left panel; error bars span
the two seeds).  The minimum at $\lambda=2.0$ and the large spread at
$\lambda=2.1$ reflect line-search thrashing, not algorithmic work that
$\lambda$ legitimately adds.}
\label{fig:lambda}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{What is \emph{not} a bottleneck.}
FEM \code{matrix\_assembly} is $0.2$--$0.9\%$ of wall time at these
mesh sizes; energy, gradient, interpolation and I/O are each a few
percent or less.  The Python-loop assembly that document
\codefile{02-phase2-timing-profile} anticipates as a future hot spot does
not bite until $10^5$--$10^6$ vertices, well beyond the $30$k reached
here.

\section{Scaling Outlook: Increasing the Number of Regions}
\label{sec:scaling}
% =========================================================================

The motivating question is how Phase~1 cost behaves as the number of
regions $N$ grows.  The projection operates on the $V\times N$ density
matrix ($V$ = vertices), so a single projection call costs
$\mathcal{O}(\code{mPII}\cdot V N)$, and total Phase~1 cost is
\begin{equation}
  T_{\text{phase 1}}
  \;\sim\;
  \underbrace{(\text{major iters})\,(\code{mBT}+1)}_{\text{\# projections}}
  \;\times\;
  \underbrace{\code{mPII}\cdot V N}_{\text{cost per projection}}.
  \label{eq:scaling}
\end{equation}
Every factor except $V$ is tied to the projection, which gives a clear
priority order for scaling up $N$:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Eliminate re-projection on rejected steps.}  Equation
        \eqref{eq:proj-count} shows the $(\code{mBT}+1)$ factor is pure
        waste in the thrashing regime.  Caching/reusing the projection
        across backtrack trials, or a step-size rule that does not thrash
        (e.g.\ Barzilai--Borwein or an adaptive \code{step0}), removes the
        single largest source of variance and the bulk of the slow-run
        cost.  Highest expected payoff.
  \item \textbf{Warm-start the projection.}  \code{mPII}$=15$--$20$ inner
        iterations per call could be cut by warm-starting
        $\Pi_{\mathcal{C}}$ from the previous accepted iterate's
        multipliers, since consecutive iterates are close.
  \item \textbf{Watch the $N$ factor directly.}  Cost per projection is
        linear in $N$, so doubling regions roughly doubles per-call cost
        \emph{at fixed mesh}.  Combined with the fact that more regions
        typically need finer meshes (larger $V$) to resolve interfaces,
        the $VN$ product is the term that will dominate at large $N$ ---
        making items~1 and~2 more, not less, important as $N$ grows.
  \item \textbf{FEM assembly is a later concern.}  Vectorising
        \code{matrix\_assembly} matters only once $V$ reaches
        $10^5$--$10^6$; it is irrelevant at the current scale.
\end{enumerate}
