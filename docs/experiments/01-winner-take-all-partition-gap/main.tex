\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Status: measured.} Phase~1 minimises a \(\Gamma\)-convergence energy over
\emph{continuous density functions} and enforces equal areas on them; the actual
partition is then extracted by a \emph{winner-take-all} (argmax) hard decision.
These two representations disagree at high \(N\): a cell can hold its full
continuous mass \(\int u_k\,dA = A/N\) exactly while its discrete winner-take-all
territory is a fraction of \(A/N\). On the torus at \(N=100\), two runs failed
Phase~2 perimeter refinement --- the perimeter \emph{rose} every iteration and the
solver stopped at a point of local infeasibility --- because one such ``runt''
cell makes the Phase~2 equal-area constraint infeasible from iteration~0. Tracking
every cell's territory across all five refinement levels shows the decisive fact:
the seeded initial condition is \emph{equally unequal} for the working \(N=50\) run
and both failing \(N=100\) runs, but the relaxation equalises the \emph{bulk} in
every case and, at \(N=100\), does so by \emph{sacrificing a single cell}. The runt
is therefore \textbf{manufactured during relaxation}, at the level where cells
condense into compact blobs, and only at high \(N\); it is not inherited from the
initial condition, and a finer mesh deepens rather than heals it.
\end{abstract}

% =====================================================================
\section{Question}
% =====================================================================
Phase~1 (the \(\Gamma\)-convergence relaxation, \codefile{src/optimization/pgd\_optimizer.py})
optimises a density field \(u\in\mathbb{R}^{V\times N}\), \(u_k(x)\in[0,1]\)
(``how strongly cell \(k\) claims vertex \(x\)''), minimising
\[
E(u)=\varepsilon\textstyle\sum_k u_k^{\top}\Ki\,u_k
 +\tfrac{1}{\varepsilon}\sum_k \big(u_k^2(1-u_k)^2\big)^{\top}\Mmat\big(u_k^2(1-u_k)^2\big)
 +\lambda\,P(u),
\]
subject to sum-to-one (\(\sum_k u_k(x)=1\)) and \textbf{equal areas}
(\(\int u_k\,dA = A/N\) for every cell), both enforced exactly at every step by
\codefile{src/optimization/projection.py}. The interface width is tied to the
mesh, \(\varepsilon=\sqrt{\text{mean triangle area}}\approx h\).

The partition that Phase~2 consumes is not the density field but its
\emph{winner-take-all} classification: each vertex is awarded to the cell of
largest density, and a cell's \emph{territory} is the area of the vertices it
wins. Nothing in \(E\) or the constraints references the argmax, so two different
``areas'' coexist: the \emph{continuous mass} \(\int u_k\,dA\) (held at \(A/N\)
exactly) and the \emph{discrete territory} (uncontrolled). For a healthy cell
(density \(\approx 1\) on a compact blob) they agree. The question: \emph{why, at
\(N=100\), do they diverge so badly that Phase~2 fails --- and where does that
divergence originate?}

% =====================================================================
\section{Method}
% =====================================================================
The discrete territory of a solution is the winner-take-all lumped-mass area:
with \(v\) the lumped P1 mass per vertex and \(w_i=\arg\max_k u_k(x_i)\), cell
\(k\)'s territory is \(a_k=\sum_{i:\,w_i=k} v_i\). We compute \(a_k\) for
(i) the final solution of each run (from \code{solution/}) and (ii) the
end-of-level density snapshot at each of the five refinement levels (from each
run's \code{traces/} HDF5), rebuilding the per-level mesh at its resolution with
\codefile{src/surfaces/torus.py}. Cell identity is preserved across levels (the
inter-level interpolation keeps the column index), so a single cell can be tracked
level to level. Two summaries per snapshot: the \emph{spread} (\(\mathrm{std}(a_k)/(A/N)\))
and the \emph{worst-cell error} (\(\max_k|a_k-A/N|/(A/N)\)).

\emph{Anchor.} The worst-cell \emph{absolute} deviation \(\max_k|a_k-A/N|\) equals
the Phase~2 equal-area constraint violation at iteration~0 (the two are the same
quantity). For the finer \(N=100\) run this reconstruction gives \(0.160\),
matching the \(1.60\mathrm{e}{-}1\) logged by Phase~2 --- confirming the mesh
rebuild and density reshape are correct before any conclusion is drawn.

\section{Results}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{The failure and its seed}
\label{sec:failure}
Both \(N=100\) runs failed Phase~2: the perimeter rose every iteration and IPOPT
stopped with ``converged to a point of local infeasibility.'' The cause is a
grossly infeasible \emph{start}: the worst cell is far from the equal-area target,
so the feasibility-first solver spends the run fighting the area imbalance ---
growing the undersized cell pushes boundaries outward, which lengthens perimeter.
Table~\ref{tab:runs} contrasts the two failures with the clean \(N=50\) run.

\subsection{The runt cell}
In the finer run, cell~92 holds its full continuous mass
(\(\int u_{92}\,dA = 0.2369 = A/N\) exactly) but wins only \(33\%\) of it as
territory (\(0.0774\)); the other \(67\%\) is a diffuse low halo over its
neighbours' ground (Fig.~\ref{fig:mass}). It is \emph{not} dormant --- peak
density \(1.0\), wins \(689\) vertices --- merely tiny on the map.

\subsection{Where the runt is born}
\label{sec:trajectory}
Tracking every cell's territory across the five levels (Fig.~\ref{fig:traj},
Table~\ref{tab:traj}) gives the central result. The \emph{spread} collapses for
all three runs (left panel) --- the relaxation equalises the bulk everywhere, at a
``condensation'' level (L1 for \(N=50\), L2 for \(N=100\)) where cells sharpen into
compact blobs. But the \emph{worst cell} (right panel) tells the opposite story at
\(N=100\): while the bulk tightens, one cell is left behind and \emph{deepens}.

Three facts follow. (1) The seeded init is \textbf{equally unequal for all three
runs} (spread \(16\)--\(19\%\), worst cell \(\sim 50\%\)) --- so the init is
\emph{not} the discriminator. (2) The relaxation equalises the \textbf{bulk} in
every run. (3) At \(N=100\) it equalises the bulk by \textbf{sacrificing one
cell}, which absorbs the residual imbalance --- permitted because the
equal-\emph{continuous}-mass constraint is satisfied by parking the lost territory
as halo. The sacrificed cell is not the init's worst cell (cell~92 was middling at
\(-27\%\) at L0; the initially-worst cell recovers), so the runt cannot be
predicted from the starting point.

\subsection{The final distribution}
The end state makes the ``single sacrifice'' explicit (Fig.~\ref{fig:dist}): at
\(N=100\) exactly one cell is a catastrophic outlier (\(-22\%\) coarse,
\(-67\%\) finer) while the other \(99\) sit within a few percent of target.

\section{Conclusion}
% =====================================================================
The high-\(N\) Phase~2 failure is a Phase~1 partition-quality problem, and the
runt is \textbf{manufactured during relaxation}, at the condensation level, and
only at high \(N\) --- not inherited from the initial condition. The driver is
\(N\) (cells shrink toward the mesh-tied interface width \(\varepsilon\)), not the
mesh: \(N=50\) and \(N=100\)-coarse share the identical finest mesh, and the finer
mesh --- with a \emph{sharper} \(\varepsilon\) --- produces a \emph{deeper} runt
(\(67\%\) vs.\ \(22\%\)), the loser of a more decisive condensation. A finer mesh
is thus the wrong lever.

\paragraph{Operating range.} The method is proven at \(N\le 50\): the \(N=50\)
control here yields a clean equal-area partition, and \(N=30\) --- stable across
seeds once seeded initialisation removed the low-\(N\) \emph{dormant} manifestation
of the same continuous-vs-discrete gap --- is the partition currently consumed by
the downstream \code{link-list-torus} project. This high-\(N\) failure is therefore
not a cosmetic edge case: it is the wall that gates scaling the pipeline past its
proven range to the region counts the downstream comparison targets. The two
manifestations are the same disease across \(N\): controlled at low \(N\) (dormant,
fixed by seeded init), it re-emerges as the runt at \(N=100\).

% =====================================================================
\section{Implications for a fix}
\label{sec:implications}
% =====================================================================
Because the relaxation \emph{actively} manufactures the runt --- nothing forbids a
cell from parking its mass as halo --- the fix must change what the optimiser is
\emph{required} to achieve, not merely its starting point. This reorders the
candidates (full ranking in the reference doc \code{winner\_take\_all\_partition\_gap.md}):
an \textbf{additional constraint} on discrete territory (a hard crispness floor, or
annealed soft-territory equality) is the front-runner; \textbf{improving the
initial condition is downgraded} (the working \(N=50\) run starts equally unequal);
soft \(\lambda\) tuning is insufficient; a seed re-roll is a non-scaling stopgap.
The cheapest next step is to prototype the crispness floor on a fast proxy and
check whether the level-2 sacrifice disappears, before spending a \(\sim 40\)~h
\(N=100\) run.
